Cloud costs have a way of growing quietly. Resources get provisioned for a specific purpose, that purpose changes, and the resources keep running. Right-sizing never quite happens because it requires time nobody has. Costs compound.

We reduced infrastructure costs by 20% on a client AWS environment without touching production reliability. Here is exactly what we did.

## Step 1: Audit what is actually running

The first step is not optimisation — it is inventory. Most AWS accounts accumulate resources that are no longer serving their original purpose.

We used AWS Cost Explorer filtered by service and tag to identify the largest cost drivers. Then we cross-referenced with CloudWatch metrics to find resources with consistently low utilisation.

**What we found:**
- 6 EC2 instances running at under 5% average CPU utilisation
- 3 RDS instances sized for peak load that never arrived
- 14 unattached EBS volumes (snapshots of deleted instances)
- 2 load balancers with no registered targets
- S3 lifecycle policies missing on buckets with growing historical data

None of this was intentional waste. It accumulated through normal project lifecycle — instances provisioned for a project that ended, databases scaled up for a load test that passed, snapshots never cleaned up.

## Step 2: Right-size over-provisioned instances

EC2 and RDS instances are commonly over-provisioned. Teams estimate peak load conservatively, provision for that estimate, and the actual workload turns out to be a fraction of the capacity.

We used AWS Compute Optimizer recommendations to identify candidates:

- 4 EC2 instances dropped from `m5.xlarge` to `m5.large` — 50% cost reduction per instance
- 2 RDS instances moved from `db.r5.large` to `db.t3.medium` — appropriate for actual query patterns
- Auto Scaling maximum values adjusted to match realistic traffic patterns

Total saving on this step: 11% of the monthly bill.

## Step 3: Clean up unused resources

The unattached EBS volumes, unused load balancers, and old snapshots were deleted after verification. This sounds obvious — it is, and yet it rarely happens without a deliberate audit.

**Process:**
1. Tag every resource with the project and team it belongs to
2. Identify untagged resources — these are the most likely to be orphaned
3. Contact the relevant team before deletion
4. Delete after confirmation (or after 30 days with no response)

Total saving: 4% of the monthly bill.

## Step 4: Add S3 lifecycle policies

S3 costs accumulate invisibly. Data written to Standard storage stays in Standard storage indefinitely unless you define a lifecycle policy.

For log buckets and backup buckets, we added:
- Transition to S3 Infrequent Access after 30 days
- Transition to S3 Glacier after 90 days
- Delete after 365 days (for log data that no longer had compliance requirements)

Total saving: 3% of the monthly bill.

## Step 5: Use Savings Plans for stable workloads

The remaining compute workload was stable enough to benefit from Compute Savings Plans — a 1-year commitment in exchange for up to 66% discount on EC2 and Lambda costs.

We committed to the baseline compute that was guaranteed to run regardless of business fluctuations. Anything that might scale down was left on On-Demand.

Total saving: 2% of the monthly bill.

## The full result

| Optimisation | Saving |
|-------------|--------|
| Right-sizing instances | 11% |
| Removing unused resources | 4% |
| S3 lifecycle policies | 3% |
| Savings Plans | 2% |
| **Total** | **20%** |

No performance impact. No reliability reduction. No capability removed.

## The ongoing practice

Cost optimisation is not a one-time project. We set up AWS Cost Anomaly Detection to alert on unexpected spending increases, scheduled a monthly Cost Explorer review, and enforced mandatory tagging for all new resources.

The 20% saving compounds. The practices prevent the waste from accumulating again.
